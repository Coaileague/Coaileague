---

**Enhancements I'd add:**

**1. State License Verification API Hook**
Instead of just accepting a license upload and trusting it, Trinity should ping the state licensing API (Texas DPS, for example) against the license number submitted and return a live status — active, expired, suspended, or not found. This turns a manual vetting step into an automated hard gate. Unlicensed applicants who check "I have a license" get flagged instantly before they even reach the interview pool.

**2. Demand-Triggered Job Publishing**
You mentioned forecasted demand and turnover — make this explicit. Trinity monitors three signals: scheduling gaps that can't be filled internally, upcoming contract start dates that need headcount, and disciplinary actions that resulted in termination. When any threshold is crossed, Trinity auto-drafts the job post and flags management to approve before publishing. Management approves in one tap, Trinity posts it. No manual job posting ever.

**3. Structured Interview Scoring Rubric**
When Trinity interviews in ChatDock, she needs a standardized question set per role type (unarmed patrol, armed, concierge, supervisor) and a scoring matrix. Each answer gets weighted — availability, prior experience, reason for leaving last employer, scenario responses. Trinity produces a numerical score plus a written summary for management to review. This protects you legally because every applicant was evaluated on the same criteria.

**4. Liability Assessment Layer**
This is your security-industry differentiator. After the interview, Trinity runs a composite liability score based on: license status (verified), any disclosed incidents or terminations, how they answered scenario-based questions (use of force judgment, chain of command), and whether uploaded documents match claimed credentials. This score goes on their applicant card visible to management.

**5. Applicant Status Pipeline (Kanban-style)**
The pool shouldn't be a flat list. It should be a pipeline with clear stages: Applied → Pre-Screened by Trinity → Interview Scheduled → Interview Complete → Management Review → Offer Extended → Onboarding → Active. Management can see every applicant's stage at a glance, and Trinity moves them through automatically until a human decision is required.

**6. Sponsorship/Training Track**
For applicants without a license, Trinity routes them into a separate track — she collects their info, explains the sponsorship process, and parks them in a "Training Pipeline" pool. When the org has bandwidth to sponsor someone, management can pull from that pool. This is a competitive advantage because most platforms ignore unlicensed applicants entirely.

**7. ChatDock Interview as Async or Scheduled**
Trinity should support both modes — she can conduct a fully async text interview (applicant answers on their own time, Trinity evaluates responses) or schedule a live session where Trinity walks them through questions in real time. Async works better for volume; live works better for supervisory roles.

---

**Architecture placement:**

This lives as a `Hiring` module inside the platform, sitting between the public-facing job board (unauthenticated) and the internal workspace (authenticated management view). Trinity connects it to three existing systems: ChatDock (for the interview), the Document Management system (for license uploads and onboarding docs), and Scheduling (for headcount demand signals). No new infrastructure needed — just new flows hung on existing rails.

---

**One honest flag:**

The public job board needs to be carefully scoped. It should be a lightweight, org-specific public page — not a full marketplace. Each org gets their own URL (e.g., `coaileague.com/jobs/statewide-protective`) and Trinity manages that page. You don't want to build an Indeed competitor — you want Trinity to be the hiring engine *behind* each org's own front door.
